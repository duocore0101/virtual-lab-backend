Result & Conclusion

Result

Arterial blood pressure (in mm of Hg) and heart rate (in beats per minute) were recorded before and after intravenous administration of various drugs. Each drug produced characteristic cardiovascular responses.

  • Adrenaline: Produced a sharp rise in both systolic and diastolic blood pressure due to combined α-mediated vasoconstriction and β1-mediated cardiac stimulation. An initial tachycardia was often followed by reflex bradycardia due to baroreceptor activation.
  • Noradrenaline: Caused a marked and sustained rise in arterial blood pressure predominantly due to strong α-adrenergic action, with prominent reflex bradycardia because of minimal β1 stimulation.
  • Acetylcholine: Produced a fall in blood pressure associated with vasodilation and cardiac inhibition, along with bradycardia due to muscarinic receptor activation. The effect is blocked by atropine.
  • Isoprenaline: Caused a fall in diastolic blood pressure due to β2-mediated vasodilation and a marked increase in heart rate due to β1 stimulation, with little change in systolic pressure.

Conclusion

The experiment clearly demonstrates that different drugs produce distinct effects on arterial blood pressure and heart rate depending on their action on α, β1, β2, and muscarinic receptors.

The observed responses highlight the role of the autonomic nervous system and baroreceptor reflex in the regulation of cardiovascular function. This study provides a clear understanding of receptor-specific drug actions on the heart and blood vessels.

Precautions

  • Ensure adequate depth of anesthesia throughout the experiment.
  • Administer drugs in correct doses and small volumes intravenously.
  • Allow sufficient washout period between successive drug injections.
  • Record blood pressure and heart rate accurately.
  • Follow CPCSEA / IAEC ethical guidelines for animal experimentation.
