Result & Conclusion

Result

The reaction time (latency to paw licking or jumping) was recorded before and after administration of the standard and test drugs using the hot plate method (55 ± 0.5°C).

Morphine (standard drug) produced a marked prolongation of latency, indicating significant analgesic activity. The test drug also showed a dose-dependent increase in reaction time when compared to the control group.

The increase in latency confirms that the observed analgesic effect is centrally mediated.

Conclusion

The hot plate method evaluates centrally acting analgesics by measuring the delay in response to a thermal nociceptive stimulus.

The significant prolongation of latency following morphine administration confirms its opioid-like central analgesic action.

A test drug showing significant and dose-dependent increase in reaction time indicates central analgesic activity.

Thus, the present experiment confirms that the analgesic effect observed is mediated through central nervous system mechanisms rather than peripheral pathways.
