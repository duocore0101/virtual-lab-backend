Theory

Introduction to Blood Withdrawal

Blood withdrawal is a fundamental laboratory technique in experimental pharmacology used to obtain blood samples for biochemical, hematological, and pharmacokinetic analysis in laboratory animals.

Purpose of Blood Collection

Blood samples are required for estimation of drug levels, metabolites, biomarkers, hormones, and safety parameters. Proper blood collection ensures reliable experimental data and reproducibility.

Types of Blood Collection Methods

Blood withdrawal techniques are classified as non-terminal (tail vein, saphenous vein, marginal ear vein) and terminal methods (cardiac puncture, posterior vena cava), depending on volume required and study design.

Animal Welfare & Ethical Considerations

All blood collection procedures must follow ethical principles including the 3Rs – Replacement, Reduction, and Refinement. Stress, pain, and blood volume limits must be strictly controlled.

Guidelines and Safety

Blood withdrawal should be performed by trained personnel under institutional and international guidelines (NC3Rs, IACUC, AVMA). Proper restraint, aseptic technique, and anesthesia (when required) are essential for animal safety.

Serum and Plasma Separation

Serum is the clear, yellowish liquid portion of blood obtained after clotting and removal of the clot. It lacks fibrinogen and other clotting factors.

Plasma is the liquid portion of blood that remains when clotting is prevented using an anticoagulant. It contains fibrinogen and clotting factors.

Both serum and plasma are obtained from whole blood after separating cellular components such as red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets through centrifugation. However, their preparation methods differ depending on whether coagulation is allowed or prevented.

Anesthesia Techniques in Experimental Animals

Anesthesia in laboratory animals, particularly rodents such as mice and rats, is essential for surgical procedures, imaging studies, and experimental interventions in pharmacological research.

Modern guidelines recommend inhalational anesthesia (e.g. isoflurane or sevoflurane) as the preferred method for recovery procedures due to its controllable depth, rapid induction, and minimal physiological disturbance.

Injectable anesthetics are also used in certain situations, particularly for short procedures. Institutional ethical committees such as IACUC require proper monitoring of respiration, body temperature, and reflexes during anesthesia.

Euthanasia Techniques in Experimental Animals

Euthanasia in laboratory animals must ensure a rapid, painless, and distress-free death while maintaining ethical standards in biomedical research.

According to the AVMA Guidelines for the Euthanasia of Animals (2020), methods are categorized as acceptable, acceptable with conditions, or unacceptable depending on animal welfare considerations.

Common euthanasia techniques in laboratory rodents include inhalation anesthetics, carbon dioxide exposure, cervical dislocation under anesthesia, and overdose of anesthetic agents. These procedures must follow institutional ethical approvals and avoid interference with experimental tissue analysis.
