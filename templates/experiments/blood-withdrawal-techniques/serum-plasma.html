Serum & Plasma Separation – Procedure

  • Collect whole blood using an appropriate blood withdrawal technique.
  • For serum: Allow blood to clot naturally at room temperature for 20–30 minutes.
  • For plasma: Collect blood into tubes containing anticoagulants (EDTA, heparin, or citrate).
  • Centrifuge samples at 3000 rpm for 10–15 minutes.
  • Carefully separate the clear supernatant without disturbing blood cells.

Post-Procedure: Store serum or plasma samples at appropriate temperatures (4°C for short term or −20°C/−80°C for long term) to preserve biochemical integrity.

Serum and Plasma Separation

Overview

Serum is the clear yellowish liquid portion of blood obtained after clotting and removal of the clot. It does not contain fibrinogen or clotting factors. Plasma is the liquid component of blood obtained when clotting is prevented using anticoagulants. Plasma contains fibrinogen and other clotting proteins. Both serum and plasma are obtained by centrifugation of whole blood to separate cellular components such as red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets.

Key Principles

  • Serum: Obtained after blood clot formation.
  • Plasma: Obtained using anticoagulant-treated blood.
  • Centrifugation: Separates cellular elements from liquid components.
  • Applications: Biochemical analysis, drug level estimation, biomarker detection.
  • Sample Integrity: Avoid hemolysis and improper storage.

Separation Processes

Serum is the clear, yellowish liquid portion of blood obtained after clotting and removal of the clot. It lacks fibrinogen and other clotting factors.

Plasma is the liquid portion of blood that remains when clotting is prevented using an anticoagulant. It contains fibrinogen and clotting factors.

Both serum and plasma are derived from whole blood after separating cellular components (red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets) through centrifugation. However, their preparation processes differ.

Preparing Serum

  1. Collect blood in a plain tube (no anticoagulant) or serum separator tube (SST).
  2. Allow blood to clot undisturbed at room temperature for 30–60 minutes.
  3. Centrifuge the sample at 1,000–2,000 × g for 10–15 minutes.
  4. The clot forms at the bottom while the clear liquid (serum) remains above it.
  5. The serum is carefully pipetted off for analysis.

Preparing Plasma

  1. Collect blood in a tube containing anticoagulant such as EDTA, heparin, or citrate.
  2. Gently invert the tube several times to mix blood with the anticoagulant.
  3. Centrifuge immediately at 1,000–2,000 × g for 10–15 minutes.
  4. The blood cells pellet at the bottom while plasma remains as the supernatant.
  5. A thin buffy coat layer containing white blood cells and platelets may appear between plasma and red blood cells.
  6. The plasma is carefully pipetted off for further biochemical or pharmacological analysis.
