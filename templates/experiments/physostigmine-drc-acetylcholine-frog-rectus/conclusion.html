Result & Conclusion

Result

The dose–response curve of acetylcholine (ACh) on isolated frog rectus abdominis muscle showed a typical sigmoid-shaped curve when plotted against the logarithm of the dose.

Increasing concentrations of acetylcholine produced graded contractions of the skeletal muscle through activation of nicotinic acetylcholine receptors.

In the presence of physostigmine, the same doses of acetylcholine produced larger contractions, indicating potentiation of the drug response.

Conclusion

Physostigmine acts as an anticholinesterase agent that inhibits the enzyme acetylcholinesterase, which normally breaks down acetylcholine.

Because acetylcholine remains longer in the tissue, the dose-response curve shifts to the left side, indicating increased potency of acetylcholine.

This leftward shift usually occurs without major change in the maximum response, demonstrating potentiation of acetylcholine action.

This experiment illustrates the role of cholinesterase in regulating acetylcholine activity at nicotinic receptor sites in skeletal muscle.
