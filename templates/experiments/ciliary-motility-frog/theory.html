Theory / Principle

Ciliary Motility in Frog Oesophagus

The frog oesophagus is lined with ciliated epithelial cells that show rhythmic, coordinated beating of cilia, which helps in the movement of particles along the mucosal surface.

Role of Acetylcholine

Ciliary beating is stimulated by acetylcholine (ACh) acting on muscarinic receptors present on the ciliated cells. This increases the frequency and efficiency of ciliary movement.

Effect of Physostigmine

Physostigmine (eserine) is a cholinesterase inhibitor that prevents the breakdown of endogenous acetylcholine, thereby potentiating the action of ACh and producing a marked increase in ciliary motility.

Effect of Atropine

Atropine is a muscarinic receptor antagonist. It blocks the action of acetylcholine on ciliated cells, resulting in decreased or complete paralysis of ciliary movement.

Assessment of Ciliary Motility

Ciliary motility is assessed indirectly by measuring the time taken for small particles (poppy seeds, cork dust, or carmine particles) to travel a fixed distance due to ciliary action. Shorter time indicates increased motility, while longer time indicates inhibition.
