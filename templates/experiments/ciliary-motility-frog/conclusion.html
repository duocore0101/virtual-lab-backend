Result & Conclusion

Result

Administration of different drugs produced distinct effects on ciliary motility of the frog oesophagus. Acetylcholine and physostigmine significantly reduced the time taken by indicator particles to travel a fixed distance, indicating enhanced ciliary activity.

Physostigmine showed the maximum stimulatory effect, whereas atropine markedly increased the transit time, indicating inhibition or paralysis of ciliary movement when compared with the control (Ringer’s solution).

Conclusion

The experiment demonstrates that parasympathomimetic drugs such as acetylcholine and physostigmine enhance ciliary motility by activating muscarinic receptors and increasing the availability of acetylcholine at the site of action.

In contrast, the parasympatholytic drug atropine inhibits ciliary movement by blocking muscarinic receptors. Physostigmine produces the strongest stimulation as it potentiates endogenous acetylcholine.

This experimental model illustrates the autonomic control of mucociliary clearance and has clinical relevance in respiratory pharmacology, particularly in conditions such as bronchitis, asthma, and disorders involving impaired mucociliary function.

Precautions

  • Maintain the oesophageal mucosa moist throughout the experiment.
  • Use indicator particles of uniform size for accurate observation.
  • Wash the preparation thoroughly between drug applications.
  • Apply drugs in appropriate concentration and sufficient contact time.
  • Follow ethical guidelines; live animal use is replaced by virtual simulation where required.
