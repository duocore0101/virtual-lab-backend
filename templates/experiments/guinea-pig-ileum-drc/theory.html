Theory / Principle

Principle of the Experiment

The guinea pig ileum is a smooth muscle preparation that is highly sensitive to several pharmacological agents. When mounted in an organ bath, the isolated ileum contracts in response to agonist drugs acting on specific receptors present on the smooth muscle.

Action of Agonists

Agonists such as Acetylcholine (ACh) bind to muscarinic M3 receptors on the smooth muscle of the ileum. Activation of these receptors leads to contraction of the longitudinal smooth muscle fibers in a concentration-dependent graded manner.

Action of Antagonists

Antagonists such as Atropine competitively block muscarinic receptors and prevent the agonist from binding. In the presence of a competitive antagonist, the dose-response curve of the agonist shifts to the right.

Competitive (Surmountable) Antagonism

Since atropine is a competitive antagonist, its inhibitory effect can be overcome by increasing the concentration of the agonist. Therefore, the maximum response remains unchanged while the EC₅₀ increases, producing a parallel rightward shift of the dose-response curve.

Recording the Response

The contraction of the ileum is recorded using a kymograph or student physiograph. The height of contraction represents the magnitude of response, which is plotted against the logarithm of drug concentration to obtain the characteristic sigmoid dose-response curve.
