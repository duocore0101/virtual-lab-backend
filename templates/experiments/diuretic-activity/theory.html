Theory / Principle

Principle of Diuretic Activity

Diuretics increase urine formation (diuresis) by acting on specific segments of the nephron to inhibit the reabsorption of sodium, chloride, and water.

Effect on Renal Tubules

Inhibition of Na+ and Cl− reabsorption results in increased excretion of electrolytes (natriuresis and chloruresis), followed by osmotic loss of water.

Lipschitz Method

The Lipschitz method (1943) is a standard screening procedure for evaluating diuretic activity in uniformly hydrated rats by comparing urine volume and electrolyte excretion between control and treated groups.

Diuretic Activity Index

Diuretic activity is calculated using the formula:

Diuretic activity =
Urine volume of test group /
Urine volume of control group

Interpretation and Classification

A value greater than 1 indicates diuresis, greater than 1.5 indicates significant activity, and values equal to or above 2 indicate a potent diuretic effect. Electrolyte ratios such as Na+/K+ help classify diuretics as loop, thiazide-like, or potassium-sparing.
