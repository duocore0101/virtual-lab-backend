Guinea Pig (Cavia porcellus)

Overview

The guinea pig (Cavia porcellus), also known as a cavy, is a widely used experimental animal in biomedical research. Unlike rats and mice, guinea pigs are hystricomorph rodents with distinct physiological traits, making them valuable for studies where murine models are inadequate. They are docile, relatively large, and share several important similarities with humans.

History and Origin

Guinea pigs were domesticated from wild cavies in South America approximately 5000–7000 years ago by Andean civilizations. They were introduced to Europe in the 16th century. Their use in laboratories began in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, with standardized strains such as Dunkin-Hartley developed in the 1920s–1930s. Major suppliers include Charles River and Inotiv.

Physical Characteristics

  • Appearance: Rounded body, short hair (common lab strains), no tail; coat colors vary (albino Hartley common)
  • Body Weight: 700–1200 g (adults)
  • Body Length: 20–30 cm
  • Lifespan: 4–8 years (longer than rats and mice)
  • Anatomy Highlights: Cannot synthesize vitamin C; sensitive hearing; four toes on forelimbs, three on hind limbs

Advantages

  • Human-like immune responses (complement system, anaphylaxis)
  • Excellent respiratory and infectious disease models
  • Only common lab rodent requiring dietary vitamin C
  • Larger size facilitates surgery and biological sampling
  • Low aggression and easy handling

Limitations

  • Higher housing and maintenance costs
  • Limited genetic tools compared to mice
  • Prone to spontaneous diseases in some strains

Common Strains

  • Dunkin-Hartley (Hartley): Outbred albino; most widely used and robust
  • Strain 2/13: Inbred; commonly used in immunological studies

Common Uses in Research

  • Immunology and allergy (anaphylaxis, asthma)
  • Infectious disease research (tuberculosis, influenza)
  • Nutrition studies (vitamin C deficiency, scurvy)
  • Audiology and otology (noise-induced hearing loss)
  • Dermatology, reproduction, and cardiovascular studies

Guinea pigs remain essential in preclinical research, particularly for respiratory and immunological studies, while emphasizing welfare through enrichment, group housing, and ethical animal experimentation.
