Cat as an Experimental Animal in Pharmacology

Overview

The domestic cat (Felis catus) has historically been used as an experimental animal in pharmacology and physiology. Due to certain physiological and neurological similarities with humans, cats were once widely employed in teaching and research. However, their status as companion animals has led to heightened ethical scrutiny and a significant reduction in their use.

Basic Details

  • Scientific Name: Felis catus
  • Common Types Used: Purpose-bred cats from licensed breeders (Class A dealers)
  • Temperament: Generally docile when properly handled

Historical Use in Pharmacology Practicals

  • Previously used in undergraduate medical and pharmacy pharmacology practicals
  • Classic experiments used anesthetized or spinal cat preparations
  • Demonstrations of blood pressure changes and autonomic nervous system drug effects
  • Widely used for nictitating membrane experiments (ganglionic blockers)
  • Used in studies of neuromuscular transmission, CNS effects, and toxicology

In older curricula, cats (along with dogs) were integral to live demonstrations in physiology and pharmacology laboratories.

Current Status in Education

  • Almost completely phased out from undergraduate teaching
  • Strict ethical regulations (e.g., CPCSEA in India) require special permissions
  • Replaced by simulations, virtual labs, videos, and clinical training
  • Focus shifted to non-animal methods aligned with the 3Rs principle

Use in Research (Preclinical / Pharmacological Studies)

Cats are still used in limited and highly regulated biomedical research, primarily where their unique physiology provides translational value.

  • Neurological research due to brain similarities with humans
  • Vision and ophthalmology studies
  • Cardiology and endocrinology research
  • Selected toxicological investigations

Purpose-bred cats are the primary source, while random-source cats are restricted or prohibited in many funded research programs.

Advantages

  • Physiological similarities to humans in certain systems
  • Useful models for neurological and vision research
  • Docile behavior with proper handling and enrichment

Ethical Considerations

Cats’ status as companion animals necessitates strict ethical oversight. All research use adheres to the 3Rs principle (Replacement, Reduction, Refinement), with emphasis on replacement in education and refinement in research through improved anesthesia, enrichment, and welfare monitoring. Non-animal alternatives are now the standard for teaching pharmacology.
