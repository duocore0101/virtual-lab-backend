Wistar Rat (Rattus norvegicus)

Overview

The Wistar rat (Rattus norvegicus) is one of the most widely used outbred albino strains in biomedical research. It serves as a versatile model organism due to its physiological similarities to humans, ease of handling, and reproducibility in experimental studies.

History and Origin

Researchers developed the Wistar rat in 1906 at the Wistar Institute, Philadelphia, USA. Physiologist Henry Herbert Donaldson, along with Helen Dean King and Milton Greenman, established the original colony from albino rats, making it the first standardized rat strain used in scientific research. Many modern laboratory rat strains, including Sprague-Dawley and Long-Evans, descend from Wistar stock.

Physical Characteristics

  • Appearance: Albino with white fur and pink/red eyes, wide head, long ears, tail shorter than body length
  • Body Weight: 200–500 g (adults)
  • Lifespan: 2–3 years (laboratory conditions)

Advantages

  • Low incidence of spontaneous tumors (notably Wistar Hannover)
  • Good survival rates in long-term studies
  • Well-documented physiological and biochemical reference data
  • Cost-effective and widely available

Limitations

  • Genetic variation may introduce experimental variability
  • Slower learning in behavioral tasks compared to Long-Evans rats

Common Uses in Research

  • Toxicology and safety testing
  • Pharmacology (drug efficacy and pharmacokinetics)
  • Oncology, neuroscience, and cardiovascular research
  • Aging, metabolic disorders, and infectious disease studies
  • Behavioral and physiological research
