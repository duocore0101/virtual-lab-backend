Hamsters as Experimental Animal Models

Overview

Hamsters are small rodents widely used in biomedical research and are distinct from mice and rats. The most common laboratory species is the Syrian (golden) hamster (Mesocricetus auratus), followed by the Chinese hamster (Cricetulus griseus) and dwarf species such as the Djungarian hamster (Phodopus sungorus). They possess unique physiological traits including cheek pouches, hibernation capability, and susceptibility to several human pathogens.

Syrian (Golden) Hamster (Mesocricetus auratus)

  • Origin: Captured from wild populations in Syria in 1930; all laboratory Syrian hamsters descend from this stock
  • Appearance: Golden/brown or cream-colored fur, large cheek pouches, robust body
  • Size: 100–150 g; lifespan 2–3 years
  • Traits: Solitary, aggressive to conspecifics, hibernation capability, short gestation (~16 days)

Advantages

  • Highly susceptible to human viruses (SARS-CoV-2, RSV, influenza)
  • Models human-like disease progression
  • Immune-privileged cheek pouches for transplant studies
  • Useful in oncology, cardiology, and metabolic research

Limitations

  • Limited genetic tools compared to mice
  • Inbred nature reduces genetic diversity
  • Prone to spontaneous diseases in aging animals

Chinese Hamster (Cricetulus griseus)

  • Appearance: Gray coat with black dorsal stripe, short tail, small cheek pouches
  • Size: ~50 g; lifespan 2–3 years
  • Genetics: Low chromosome number (2n = 22)

Advantages

  • Source of CHO cells for biopharmaceutical production
  • Produces complex human-like glycosylated proteins
  • Used in toxicology, genetics, and diabetes research

Limitations

  • Less common as a whole-animal model
  • Lower reproductive performance than Syrian hamsters

Djungarian (Siberian) Hamster (Phodopus sungorus)

  • Appearance: Small body, gray coat with winter white color change
  • Size: 20–50 g
  • Traits: Photoperiod-sensitive reproduction and metabolism

Djungarian hamsters are mainly used in studies of circadian rhythm, seasonal biology, metabolism, and behavior. They are less susceptible to some viral infections compared to Syrian hamsters.

Common Uses in Research

  • Infectious diseases (COVID-19, influenza, leishmaniasis)
  • Biopharmaceutical production (CHO cells)
  • Oncology and toxicology
  • Metabolic, reproductive, and circadian rhythm studies

Hamsters complement mice and rats in research by modeling human-relevant pathogenesis, while adhering to ethical standards in animal experimentation. Syrian hamsters remain the most versatile whole-animal hamster model.
