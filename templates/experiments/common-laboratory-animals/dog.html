Dog as an Experimental Animal in Pharmacology

Overview

The dog (Canis familiaris) has historically played a significant role in pharmacology education and research. Purpose-bred Beagle dogs are the most commonly used due to their medium size, docile temperament, ease of handling, genetic uniformity, and extensive historical reference data. Although largely phased out from undergraduate teaching, dogs remain important in specific preclinical research applications.

Basic Details

  • Scientific Name: Canis familiaris
  • Common Research Breed: Beagle
  • Adult Body Weight: ~10–15 kg
  • Temperament: Docile, cooperative, and trainable

Historical Use in Pharmacology Practicals

  • Widely used in undergraduate medical and pharmacy pharmacology labs
  • Classic experiments involved anesthetized dogs for recording blood pressure and heart rate
  • Demonstration of drug effects such as adrenaline, acetylcholine, histamine, and autonomic agents
  • Used for studying hypoglycemic agents, anti-arrhythmics, CNS drugs, and routes of administration

Prior to the 2000s, dogs (along with cats) were commonly used for live demonstrations in physiology and pharmacology teaching.

Current Status in Education

  • Largely phased out from undergraduate teaching due to ethical concerns
  • Strict animal welfare regulations (e.g., CPCSEA in India)
  • Replaced by computer simulations, videos, mannequins, and case-based learning
  • Live animal use is no longer required or common in student practicals

Use in Research (Preclinical / Pharmacological Studies)

Despite reduced use in education, dogs continue to be important in advanced pharmacological and toxicological research, particularly as a required non-rodent species for regulatory drug development.

  • Cardiovascular safety pharmacology (heart rate, blood pressure, QT interval)
  • Toxicology and pharmacokinetics studies
  • Models for diabetes mellitus, CNS drugs, and gastrointestinal diseases
  • Organ transplantation and safety assessment studies

Advantages

  • Physiological similarities to humans, especially cardiovascular and gastrointestinal systems
  • Cooperative behavior and ease of training
  • Extensive historical baseline data for comparison
  • Beagles are internationally accepted for regulatory studies

Ethical Considerations

The use of dogs in pharmacology strictly follows the 3Rs principle—Replacement, Reduction, and Refinement. In teaching, replacement with simulations is now standard. In research, dog use is permitted only when scientifically justified and conducted under strict ethical and regulatory oversight.
