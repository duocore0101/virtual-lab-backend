Swiss Albino Mice

Overview

Swiss albino mice (Mus musculus) are a widely used outbred albino strain in biomedical research. They are general-purpose laboratory animals valued for genetic heterogeneity, vigorous reproduction, robustness, and cost-effectiveness, particularly in pharmacology and toxicology studies.

History and Origin

The Swiss albino strain originated in 1926 when Clara J. Lynch imported a small colony of non-inbred albino mice from Lausanne, Switzerland, to the Rockefeller Institute, New York. These mice formed the foundation for commercial outbred stocks such as Swiss Webster, CD-1, CFW, and RjHan:SWISS.

Physical Characteristics

  • Appearance: Albino, white fur, pink/red eyes
  • Body Weight: 20–40 g (adult)
  • Lifespan: 1.5–3 years (laboratory)

Advantages

  • Represents human population variability
  • Hardy, vigorous, excellent breeders
  • Cost-effective and widely available
  • Good sentinels for pathogen monitoring

Limitations

  • Higher experimental variability
  • Light sensitivity due to albinism
  • Limited advanced genetic tools
