Sprague-Dawley Rat (Rattus norvegicus)

Overview

The Sprague-Dawley rat (Rattus norvegicus), commonly abbreviated as SD rat, is one of the most popular outbred albino strains used in biomedical research. It is a versatile, multipurpose model valued for its calm temperament, ease of handling, rapid growth, and strong physiological relevance to humans.

History and Origin

The Sprague-Dawley rat was developed in 1925 by Robert Dawley at the Sprague-Dawley Animal Company in Madison, Wisconsin. It originated from a hybrid hooded male crossed with a female Wistar rat. The strain was later refined and commercialized, with widespread distribution beginning in the 1950s. Modern Sprague-Dawley rats are supplied by vendors such as Charles River, Taconic, and Inotiv, with minor genetic differences due to separate breeding colonies.

Physical Characteristics

  • Appearance: Albino with white fur and pink/red eyes; longer body and tail compared to Wistar rats
  • Body Weight: Females 250–500 g; males 300–800 g
  • Lifespan: 2–3.5 years (laboratory conditions)

Advantages

  • Rapid growth and larger size suitable for surgery and imaging
  • Excellent reproductive performance and hardiness
  • Extensive historical data on physiology, toxicology, and behavior
  • Widely available from multiple suppliers

Limitations

  • Higher incidence of spontaneous tumors than some strains
  • Genetic variability between vendors affecting reproducibility
  • Prone to obesity on high-fat diets

Common Uses in Research

  • Toxicology and safety assessment
  • Pharmacology, pharmacokinetics, and drug development
  • Metabolic disorders (obesity and diabetes models)
  • Neuroscience, cardiovascular, and oncology research
  • Surgical, nutritional, and aging studies
