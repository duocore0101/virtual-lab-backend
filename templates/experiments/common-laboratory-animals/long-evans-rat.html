Long-Evans Rat (Rattus norvegicus)

Overview

The Long-Evans rat (Rattus norvegicus), also known as the hooded rat, is a popular outbred strain in biomedical research. It is distinguished by its pigmented hood and superior visual acuity, making it especially valuable for studies involving learning, behavior, and vision when compared to albino rat strains.

History and Origin

The Long-Evans rat was developed in 1915 by Drs. Joseph Abraham Long and Herbert McLean Evans at the University of California, Berkeley. It was created by crossing female Wistar albino rats with a wild gray male Rattus norvegicus, aiming to produce a vigorous and hardy strain. The inclusion of wild genetics contributed to enhanced sensory and behavioral traits. The strain was later distributed by suppliers such as Charles River and Taconic.

Physical Characteristics

  • Appearance: White body with a pigmented hood (black or brown) over the head and shoulders; pigmented eyes
  • Body Weight: 200–400 g (adults; males larger)
  • Lifespan: 2.5–3.5 years (laboratory conditions)

Advantages

  • Pigmented eyes provide superior vision and reduced light sensitivity
  • Excellent learners in spatial and operant conditioning tasks
  • Higher voluntary alcohol consumption (useful for addiction studies)
  • Low spontaneous tumor incidence and good respiratory health
  • Hyperactive HPA axis, useful for stress-related research

Limitations

  • Smaller size may limit certain surgical procedures
  • Potential variability in activity or aggression levels

Comparison with Wistar and Sprague-Dawley Rats

Common Uses in Research

  • Behavioral neuroscience (learning, memory, spatial navigation)
  • Addiction studies (especially alcohol preference)
  • Vision and ophthalmology research
  • Stress, anxiety, and maternal behavior models
  • Diet-induced obesity, toxicology, and aging studies

Derivative models include Long-Evans Cinnamon (Wilson’s disease) and Otsuka-Long-Evans-Tokushima Fatty rats (diabetes).
