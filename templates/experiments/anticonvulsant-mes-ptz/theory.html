Theory: Anticonvulsant Activity by MES or PTZ Method

A) Maximal Electroshock Seizure (MES) Method

The Maximal Electroshock Seizure (MES) method is one of the most widely used experimental models for screening anticonvulsant drugs effective against generalized tonic–clonic (grand mal) seizures.

In this method, a supramaximal electrical stimulus is delivered through corneal or ear clip electrodes to experimental animals. This produces a characteristic sequence of seizure phases, the most important being hind limb tonic extension (HLTE).

Anticonvulsant drugs that are effective in this model prevent the spread of seizure activity in the brain and abolish hind limb tonic extension. Such drugs are considered useful in the treatment of tonic–clonic seizures in humans.

  • MES simulates generalized tonic–clonic seizures
  • Key endpoint: abolition of hind limb tonic extension (HLTE)
  • Predictive of clinical efficacy against grand mal epilepsy

Mechanism of Action (MES Model)

Seizure activity produced by maximal electroshock spreads through brainstem centers. Drugs that block this spread are effective in abolishing tonic extension.

Most drugs effective in the MES model act by blocking voltage-gated sodium channels, thereby preventing sustained neuronal firing.

  • Blocks seizure propagation
  • Acts mainly via sodium channel inhibition
  • Examples: Phenytoin, Carbamazepine, Lamotrigine

B) Pentylenetetrazol (PTZ) Induced Seizure Method

The Pentylenetetrazol (PTZ) induced seizure method is used to evaluate anticonvulsant drugs effective against clonic, myoclonic, and absence (petit mal) seizures.

PTZ is a chemical convulsant that lowers the seizure threshold by antagonizing inhibitory neurotransmission in the central nervous system. Administration of PTZ produces myoclonic jerks followed by generalized clonic seizures in experimental animals.

  • Models absence and myoclonic seizures
  • Induces clonic convulsions
  • Useful for screening GABA-enhancing drugs

Mechanism of Action (PTZ Model)

PTZ induces seizures by antagonizing the GABAA receptor, which reduces inhibitory neurotransmission in the brain and increases neuronal excitability.

Drugs that enhance GABAergic transmission increase seizure threshold, delay the onset of seizures, or completely prevent convulsions in this model.

  • Acts by blocking GABAA receptors
  • Produces clonic seizures
  • Standard drug: Diazepam

Comparison of MES and PTZ Models

  • MES: Screens drugs effective against tonic–clonic seizures
  • PTZ: Screens drugs effective against clonic and absence seizures
  • Activity in both models suggests broad-spectrum anticonvulsant potential
