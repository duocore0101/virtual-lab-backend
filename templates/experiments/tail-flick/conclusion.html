Result & Conclusion

Result

The tail withdrawal latency was recorded before and after administration of standard and test drugs using the tail flick method (radiant heat stimulus).

Morphine (standard drug) produced a marked prolongation of latency, confirming significant central (opioid-like) analgesic activity.

The test drug showed a dose-dependent increase in reaction time compared to the control group, indicating central analgesic effect.

Conclusion

The tail flick method evaluates centrally acting analgesics by measuring the latency of a spinal nociceptive reflex following radiant heat stimulation.

Significant prolongation of tail withdrawal latency confirms central analgesic activity, particularly opioid-mediated mechanisms.

The tail flick method is often more specific for strong opioid analgesics and is highly effective for evaluating spinal-level analgesic responses.

Thus, the present experiment confirms that the analgesic effect observed is centrally mediated rather than peripheral.
