Theory / Principle

Principle

When mydriatic or miotic drugs are instilled into the rabbit eye, they interact with specific receptors present on the iris muscles, resulting in changes in pupil diameter.

The extent and duration of pupillary response depend on the type of drug used, its mechanism of action, and the receptors involved. These changes can be easily observed and measured in the rabbit eye.

Mydriatics (Pupil Dilators)

Mydriatic drugs produce dilation of the pupil either by stimulating sympathetic activity or by blocking parasympathetic activity.

  • Parasympatholytic (Anticholinergic): Atropine, Homatropine, Tropicamide, Cyclopentolate block muscarinic (M₃) receptors, causing relaxation of the sphincter pupillae and abolition of light reflex (cycloplegia).
  • Sympathomimetic: Phenylephrine and Adrenaline stimulate α₁ receptors on the dilator pupillae muscle, producing dilation while preserving the light reflex.

Miotics (Pupil Constrictors)

Miotic drugs cause constriction of the pupil (miosis) by contracting the sphincter pupillae muscle through stimulation of muscarinic receptors.

  • Direct-acting cholinergics: Pilocarpine directly stimulates muscarinic (M₃) receptors, producing miosis and exaggeration of light reflex.
  • Indirect-acting cholinergics: Physostigmine and Neostigmine inhibit acetylcholinesterase, increasing acetylcholine levels and enhancing parasympathetic effects on the eye.

Mechanism of Receptor Interaction

The iris contains two muscles:

  • Sphincter pupillae – controlled by parasympathetic (M₃) receptors
  • Dilator pupillae – controlled by sympathetic (α₁) receptors

Depending on whether parasympathetic activity is blocked or stimulated, the pupil undergoes dilation (mydriasis) or constriction (miosis).

Significance

The rabbit eye serves as a reliable experimental model for studying autonomic drugs. Observation of pupil size and light reflex helps in understanding receptor mechanisms and differentiating between mydriatic and miotic drugs.

These observations have important clinical relevance in glaucoma management, ophthalmic examinations, and disorders of the autonomic nervous system.
