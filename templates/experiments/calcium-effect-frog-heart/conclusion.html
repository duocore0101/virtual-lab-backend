Result & Conclusion

Result & Discussion

  • Positive Inotropic Effect: An increase in extracellular Ca²⁺ increases the concentration gradient, leading to greater calcium influx during the cardiac action potential. This saturates Troponin-C binding sites and increases the force of contraction.
  • Systolic Arrest (Calcium Rigor): At very high calcium concentrations, the heart fails to relax and stops in a fully contracted state.
    • Mechanism: Relaxation requires removal of Ca²⁺ from the cytoplasm using ATP. Excess intracellular calcium exceeds the pumping capacity, resulting in sustained actin–myosin cross-bridge formation (rigor).
  • Diastolic Arrest: In the absence of calcium (calcium-free Ringer’s solution), excitation–contraction coupling cannot occur. The heart becomes flaccid and stops in the relaxed (diastolic) state.

Conclusion

  • Calcium ions are essential for cardiac muscle contraction.
  • A moderate increase in Ca²⁺ produces a stronger and faster heartbeat (positive inotropic and chronotropic effects).
  • Excessive Ca²⁺ leads to cardiac arrest in systole (calcium rigor).
  • Absence of Ca²⁺ results in cardiac arrest in diastole.
