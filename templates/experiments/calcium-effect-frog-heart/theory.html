{% load static %} Effect of Calcium Ions on Frog’s Heart – Theory

To study the effect of varying concentrations of calcium ions on frog’s heart

Principle

The frog heart muscle (myocardium) acts as a functional syncytium. Unlike skeletal muscle, cardiac muscle depends heavily on extracellular calcium ions (Ca²⁺) for contraction.

Therefore, variations in calcium concentration in the perfusion fluid directly influence both heart rate (chronotropy) and force of contraction (inotropy).

Excitation–Contraction Coupling

  • During the plateau phase (Phase 2) of the cardiac action potential, calcium enters the cardiomyocyte through L-type voltage-gated calcium channels.
  • This calcium influx triggers release of additional calcium from the sarcoplasmic reticulum (Calcium-Induced Calcium Release).
  • Released calcium binds to Troponin-C, initiating actin–myosin interaction and cardiac contraction.

Role of Extracellular Calcium

Cardiac muscle contraction strength is directly proportional to the amount of calcium available extracellularly.

Increasing calcium concentration enhances calcium influx, producing a positive inotropic effect, while calcium deficiency reduces myocardial contractility.

Frog Heart Specificity

The sarcoplasmic reticulum in the frog heart is poorly developed compared to mammals.

Hence, the frog heart is highly sensitive to changes in the calcium concentration of the perfusion fluid.

Excess calcium may lead to sustained contraction (systolic arrest), whereas calcium deficiency causes weak or absent contractions.
