{% load static %} Effect of Adrenaline on Frog’s Heart – Theory

To study the effect of adrenaline on frog’s heart

Principle

Adrenaline binds primarily to β1-adrenergic receptors on cardiac myocytes in the frog heart, producing:

  • Positive chronotropic effect – Increase in heart rate (tachycardia)
  • Positive inotropic effect – Increase in force of contraction (increased amplitude)

Mechanism of Action

  1. Activation of β1 receptors → Coupling to Gs proteins → Stimulation of adenylyl cyclase → Increased cAMP → Activation of protein kinase A
  2. Phosphorylation of calcium channels → Increased Ca²⁺ influx → Enhanced contractility
  3. Acceleration of pacemaker activity in the sinus venosus → Increased heart rate

The effect is dose-dependent, reversible on washing with Ringer’s solution, and blocked by β-blockers (e.g., propranolol).

Adrenergic Action on Heart

Adrenaline and nor-adrenaline act as neurotransmitters at sympathetic sites and in some areas of the brain.

Adrenaline, like sympathomimetics, increases heart rate by activating the SA node (pacemaker).

Effect on Pacemakers and Contractility

Both adrenaline and nor-adrenaline activate latent pacemakers such as AV node and Purkinje’s fibers.

Adrenaline increases the force of cardiac contraction by acting on β1 adrenoceptors.

Increased permeability to Ca²⁺ and Na⁺ produces an excitatory effect resulting in increased heart rate and force of contraction.
