Result & Discussion

A. Diastolic Arrest (Excess Potassium)

When extracellular potassium concentration increases, the potassium concentration gradient across the cardiac cell membrane decreases.

  • Depolarization: The resting membrane potential becomes less negative (partial depolarization).
  • Inactivation: Sustained partial depolarization inactivates voltage-gated sodium (Na⁺) channels. Without functional Na⁺ channels, the heart cannot generate a new action potential.
  • Result: The heart loses excitability and fails to contract, stopping in a relaxed, dilated state known as diastolic arrest.

B. Systolic Arrest (Lack of Potassium)

When extracellular potassium is removed using potassium-free Ringer’s solution, the Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase pump is inhibited because it requires extracellular potassium for normal function.

  • Sodium Accumulation: Intracellular sodium accumulates as it is not pumped out of the cell.
  • Calcium Overload: Elevated intracellular sodium inhibits the Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger (NCX), preventing calcium extrusion and causing intracellular calcium to rise.
  • Result: Excess intracellular calcium produces sustained contraction (rigor), similar to the effect of digitalis or excess calcium, leading to systolic arrest.

Conclusion

  • Excess potassium acts as a cardiac depressant, causing the heart to stop in diastole (relaxation).
  • Lack of potassium paradoxically increases muscle tone due to inhibition of membrane pumps and calcium accumulation, eventually causing the heart to stop in systole (contraction).
