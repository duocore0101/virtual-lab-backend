Theory/Principle

Role of Mast Cells in Allergy

Mast cells play a major role in allergic reactions. They are immune cells present in connective tissues and are rich in granules containing inflammatory mediators.

Mast Cell Degranulation

On exposure to allergens or chemical agents, mast cells release inflammatory mediators such as:

  • Histamine
  • Leukotrienes
  • Prostaglandins

This process is known as mast cell degranulation.

Mechanism of Anti-Allergic Drugs

Drugs with anti-allergic activity stabilize the mast cell membrane and prevent the release of inflammatory mediators.

This stabilization reduces allergic responses such as inflammation, itching, and bronchoconstriction.

Evaluation of Mast Cell Stabilization

The extent of mast cell stabilization can be evaluated by:

  • Inducing degranulation using an agent (Compound 48/80 or egg albumin)
  • Counting intact and degranulated mast cells microscopically
  • Calculating percentage (%) protection

Interpretation

A decrease in the number of degranulated mast cells compared to disease control indicates mast cell stabilizing (anti-degranulation) activity.

Higher percentage protection suggests stronger anti-allergic potential of the test drug.
