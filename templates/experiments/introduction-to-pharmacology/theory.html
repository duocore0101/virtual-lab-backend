Theory / Principle

1. Definition and Etymology

Pharmacology: Derived from Greek words "Pharmakon" (drug or poison) and "Logos" (study or discourse). It is the scientific study of drugs including their history, sources, physicochemical properties, physiological effects, mechanism of action, absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion (ADME), and therapeutic or toxic effects.

Experimental Pharmacology: The branch focused on studying drug effects using laboratory models (in vivo, in vitro, ex vivo, or simulated). It is a relatively young discipline enriched by advances in electrophysiology, biochemistry, molecular biology, and digital recording systems.

2. Key Definitions (Basic Terminology)

  • Drug: Any active chemical substance used for diagnosis, prevention, relief, treatment, or cure of diseases in humans or animals.
  • Medicine: Formulation containing the drug plus excipients (binders, lubricants, etc.) for stable and acceptable delivery.
  • Pharmacokinetics (PK): Study of what the body does to the drug (absorption, distribution, metabolism, elimination).
  • Pharmacodynamics (PD): Study of what the drug does to the body (mechanism and site of action).
  • Bioavailability: Fraction of unchanged drug that reaches systemic circulation after administration.
  • Other terms: Clinical pharmacology, Neuropharmacology, Pharmacogenetics, Posology, Pharmacovigilance, Side effects vs Adverse effects.

3. Scope and Importance in Pharmacy

  • Experimental pharmacology bridges theoretical knowledge with practical application.
  • It helps in drug discovery, development, safety evaluation, and understanding therapeutic uses.
  • For pharmacy students it supports rational drug use, formulation development, clinical pharmacy and regulatory understanding.
  • In India, drug research and experiments are regulated under authorities such as CDSCO and CPCSEA.
  • As per modern PCI guidelines, many live animal experiments are replaced by simulations, videos and virtual laboratory platforms.

4. Aims / Objectives of Experimental Pharmacology

The main goals are:

  1. To screen and identify new therapeutic agents suitable for human use (drug discovery).
  2. To study toxicity, safety and possible side effects of drugs.
  3. To elucidate the mechanism and site of drug action.
  4. To perform bioassays and evaluate drug potency and efficacy.
  5. To support preclinical drug development before clinical trials.

5. Stages of Drug Evaluation

A.Preclinical experimental pharmacology involves identification and optimization of lead compounds and testing them in animals, isolated tissues, organs or cell cultures.

Why Preclinical Studies are Done?

  • To check if the drug is safe for humans.
  • To evaluate whether the drug is effective against the disease.
  • To study how the body handles the drug (ADME).
  • To determine appropriate dose ranges and side effects.
  • To generate data required for IND application to CDSCO.

Main Types of Preclinical Studies

  • Efficacy / Pharmacological Screening
  • ADME / Pharmacokinetic Studies
  • Toxicity Studies (Acute, Sub-acute, Chronic, Genotoxicity, Carcinogenicity)
  • Safety Pharmacology

Methods Used

  • In vitro: Cells or isolated tissues
  • In vivo: Whole animal studies
  • Ex vivo: Removed tissues
  • In silico: Computer simulations

All studies follow Good Laboratory Practice (GLP) and the 3R principles.

Outcome Successful preclinical data is submitted to CDSCO as Investigational New Drug (IND) application to get permission for human (clinical) trials.

5. Stages of Drug Evaluation

B.Clinical Pharmacology: Testing on human volunteers and patients (Phases I–IV) to evaluate pharmacokinetics, safety, efficacy, and post-marketing surveillance.

Developing a new drug from an idea in the laboratory to a medicine available for patients is a long, expensive and highly regulated journey. It usually takes 10–15 years and costs approximately ₹8,000–15,000 crore (US$ 1–2 billion). Only about 1 out of 5,000–10,000 screened compounds finally reaches the market. Experimental pharmacology plays a major role especially during the preclinical stage.

Main Stages of Drug Discovery and Development

  1. Target Identification & Validation: Scientists identify a biological target (such as a receptor, enzyme, or gene) linked to a disease and confirm that modifying this target may treat the disease.
  2. Hit Discovery: Thousands to millions of chemical compounds are screened (using High-Throughput Screening or computer-based methods) to identify “hits” showing activity against the target.
  3. Lead Identification & Optimization: Promising hits are chemically modified to improve potency, selectivity, safety, and pharmacokinetic properties (ADME).
  4. Preclinical Studies (Core of Experimental Pharmacology): The optimized lead compound is tested in the laboratory using:
    • In vitro studies (cells or isolated tissues)
    • In vivo studies (animal models)
    • ADME studies
    • Toxicity and safety testing
    This stage generates the essential data required before human trials.
  5. IND Application: Preclinical data is submitted to the regulatory authority (CDSCO in India) as an Investigational New Drug (IND) application to obtain permission for human studies.
  6. Clinical Trials:
    • Phase I: Safety, dosage and side effects tested in 20–100 healthy volunteers.
    • Phase II: Efficacy and side effects studied in 100–500 patients.
    • Phase III: Large confirmatory trials in 1,000–5,000 patients to confirm safety and efficacy.
  7. Regulatory Review & Approval: All data is submitted as a New Drug Application (NDA). If approved by CDSCO, the drug can be marketed.
  8. Phase IV (Post-Marketing Surveillance / Pharmacovigilance): Continuous monitoring after marketing to detect rare or long-term adverse effects.

6. Ethical Aspects and Guidelines

  • Experiments must follow CPCSEA guidelines for humane use of animals.
  • The key ethical principles are the 4Rs: Replacement, Reduction, Refinement and Rehabilitation.
  • All studies must be approved by the Institutional Animal Ethics Committee (IAEC).
  • Proper maintenance, breeding, housing and record keeping of laboratory animals is essential.
  • Detailed CPCSEA guidelines are often discussed in a separate experiment.

7. Types of Experiments in Pharmacology

  • In vivo: Whole animal experiments.
  • In vitro: Experiments on isolated tissues or organs.
  • Ex vivo: Experiments on tissues removed from animals.
  • Simulated / Virtual: Computer or software-based experiments as recommended by PCI.

Common experimental models include:

  • Mice or rats – CNS and behavioral studies
  • Frogs – muscle or ciliary motility studies
  • Rabbits – eye or insulin studies
