Theory / Principle

1. Introduction

Experimental animals have been of very important tools in the history of non-human research models for scientific purposes in almost every aspect of biomedical, behavioral researches and testing conducted in Universities, Medical schools, Pharmaceutical companies, research institutes, farms and commercial facilities that provide animal-testing services to industry. Experiments on animals are necessary in drugs discovery and development as well as to advance medical and biological knowledge.

Dosage calculation and stock solution preparation based on dosage rationale formula are prerequisites to drug administration in experimental animals. However, drugs dosage calculations and stock solution preparations are not clearly explained in most scientific literatures involving the use of experimental animals, and this is a major challenge to some undergraduate students, post-graduate students and other researchers.

Since over 90% of animals used in in vivo experiments in medical, physiological, pharmacological, chemical, toxicological, biological, biochemical and genetic studies are rats and mice, this work is aimed to simplify calculation of doses, preparation of stock solution in experimental animal for the benefits of all researchers.

2. Vehicle of Choice, Drugs Dissolution and Volume Selection Rationale

A vehicle is any substance that acts as a medium in which a drug is administered. Vehicle, which is an essential consideration in all animal research should be biologically inert, have no toxic effects on the animals and not also influence the results obtained for the compound under investigation.

Example of suitable vehicles for animal research include; water, normal saline (0.9% sodium chloride), 50% polyethylene glycol, 5 to 10% Tween 80, 0.25% methylcellulose or carboxymethylcellulose.

In most researches involving experimental animals, dosages are usually calculated from stock solution of the test drugs dissolved in appropriate volume of solvent (vehicle). According to the OECD’s (Organization of Economic Cooperation and Development) guidelines, dosage of drug (mg) should be constituted in an appropriate volume not usually exceeding 10 ml/kg (1 ml/100g) body weight of experimental animals (mice and rats) for non-aqueous solvent in oral route of administration.

However, in the case of aqueous solvents, 20 ml/kg (2 ml/100g) body weight can be considered (OECD, 2000). Large dose volumes (40 ml/kg body weight) can cause unnecessary stress to animals and can overload the stomach capacity, resulting in reflux, aspiration pneumonia, pharyngeal, esophageal and gastric irritation or rupture. Lower volume (5 ml/kg) can be considered to dissolve highly soluble drugs. However, final dilution volume should not exceed 20 ml/kg.

Based on 10 ml/kg volume selection, required dose volume for a 100 g rat: (100 g = 0.1 kg) × 10 ml = 1 ml.
NB: 1 kg = 1000 g.

Based on 20 ml/kg volume selection, required dose volume for a 100 g rat: (0.1 kg) × 20 ml = 2 ml.

This is illustrated below in the OECD guideline table on volume selection.

3. Dosage Calculation and Preparation of Stock Solution of Crude Plant Extract

With reference to table 1 above, stock solutions and doses of a plant extract (with selected doses 200 mg/kg and 400 mg/kg) for a rat weighing 120 g can be calculated as follows:

Step 1: Dosage Calculation

Body weight of animal = 120 g = 0.12 kg

Dosage in mg = Body weight (kg) × Dose (mg/kg)

Dosage in mg = 0.12 × 200 mg = 24 mg

Step 2: Dissolution in Suitable Vehicle

According to OECD guidelines, a 120 g rat requires 24 mg of crude plant extract constituted in not more than 1.2 ml of normal saline (10 ml/kg rule).

In summary: 120 g ≡ 24 mg ≡ 1.2 ml of normal saline

Bulk stock preparation:

24 mg = 1.2 ml
40 × 24 mg = 40 × 1.2 ml
960 mg dissolved in 48 ml normal saline
= 20 mg/ml stock solution

From this stock (20 mg/ml): 1 ml is required for 100 g rat, while 1.2 ml is required for 120 g rat.

For higher selected dose (400 mg/kg): 960 mg dissolved in 24 ml → 40 mg/ml which is twice the concentration of the 200 mg/kg stock.

The above table shows that animals with higher body weight in the same standard dose category will receive higher doses (mg or ml) of plant extract solution. Also, the stock solution for high dose will be thicker and more concentrated than low dose.

4. Dosage Calculation and Preparation of Stock Solution of a Reference Drug (Silymarin)

Silymarin (figure 1) is a reference drug (with dosage formulations of 70 mg and 140 mg per tablet respectively) used in animal models for screening hepatoprotective, nephroprotective and antioxidant agents at standard doses ranging between 25 mg/kg to 200 mg/kg body weight.

Figure 1: Silymarin Tablet

Example Calculation:

Required dose of silymarin (70 mg tablet) for a rat weighing 130 g at a standard dose of 25 mg/kg:

Step 1: Dosage Calculation

Required dose = Body weight (kg) × Standard dose (mg/kg)

0.13 × 25 mg = 3.25 mg

Step 2: Dissolution in Suitable Vehicle

A 130 g rat requires 3.25 mg of silymarin and this dose should be constituted in not more than 1.3 ml of normal saline according to OECD’s 10 ml/kg guideline.

In summary: 130 g ≡ 3.25 mg ≡ 1.3 ml of normal saline

If 3.25 mg is constituted in 1.3 ml, then one tablet of silymarin (70 mg) would be constituted in:

(1.3 ml ÷ 3.25 mg) × 70 mg = 28 ml of normal saline

Therefore stock solution: 70 mg / 28 ml = 2.5 mg/ml

From this stock solution, dosages can be administered to animals of varying body weights based on the OECD 10 ml/kg standard volume rationale as shown in Table 3 below.

= (2.5 mg/ml) 		130 g 3.25 mg 1.30 ml

5. Dosage Calculation and Preparation of Stock Solution for Salt Compound (Alloxan Monohydrate)

Alloxan monohydrate is a diabetogenic agent used at a standard dose of 150 mg/kg for rats via the intraperitoneal (IP) route as reported in several experimental diabetes literatures (Erhirhie et al., 2013; Oluwole et al., 2012). It is available in salt form.

The required dose of alloxan monohydrate to induce experimental diabetes intraperitoneally in a rat weighing 200 g at a standard dose of 150 mg/kg can be calculated as follows:

Step 1: Dosage Calculation

Required dose = Body weight (kg) × Standard dose (mg/kg)

0.2 × 150 mg = 30 mg

Step 2: Volume Selection for IP Route

For intraperitoneal administration, an appropriate vehicle volume ranging between 2 ml/kg to 5 ml/kg in rats is recommended.

Based on 2 ml/kg volume selection:

Required volume = 0.2 kg × 2 ml = 0.4 ml

Therefore, 30 mg (0.03 g) of alloxan monohydrate should be constituted in 0.4 ml of normal saline.

Bulk Stock Preparation

For multiple animals, stock can be prepared by weighing:

300 mg (0.3 g) dissolved in 4 ml normal saline

Concentration = 300 mg / 4 ml = 75 mg/ml

Standard Dose Stock Solution Animal Body Weight (g) Calculated Dose (mg) Equivalent Dose (ml)
Alloxan Monohydrate, 150 mg/kg 300 mg / 4 ml
= (75 mg/ml) 		200 g 30 mg 0.40 ml
180 g 27 mg 0.36 ml
160 g 24 mg 0.32 ml
150 g 22.5 mg 0.30 ml

From the above table, animals with higher body weight will receive higher doses (mg or ml) of alloxan monohydrate solution.

Larger volume of stock solution can be prepared for multiple animals by dissolving:
600 mg (0.6 g) in 8 ml normal saline, or 1200 mg (1.2 g) in 16 ml normal saline.

Analytical weighing balance with high accuracy and sensitivity should be used when weighing alloxan monohydrate.

For mice, dissolution volume of 5 ml/kg can be considered due to their lower body weight compared to rats.

6. Dosage Calculation and Preparation of Stock Solution for Ampoules (Gentamicin Injection)

Gentamicin (figure 2) is an aminoglycoside antibiotic used to induce nephrotoxicity in experimental animals (rats and mice) at a standard dose of 80 mg/kg via intraperitoneal route as reported in literature.

Figure 2: Gentamicin Injection (80 mg/2 ml)

The required dose of gentamicin to induce experimental nephrotoxicity intraperitoneally in a rat weighing 100 g at a standard dose of 80 mg/kg can be calculated as follows:

Step 1: Dosage Calculation

Required dose = Body weight (kg) × Standard dose (mg/kg)

0.1 × 80 mg = 8 mg

Step 2: Required Volume Calculation

Gentamicin ampoule contains 80 mg / 2 ml which equals 40 mg/ml.

Thus: 40 mg ≡ 1 ml 8 mg ≡ (8 ÷ 40) ml = 0.2 ml

Therefore, 100 g rat requires 0.2 ml of gentamicin injection.

Required dosages for animals of varying body weights are shown below.

Gentamicin, 80 mg/kg (Rats) 80 mg / 2 ml
= (40 mg/ml) 		100 g 8 mg 0.20 ml
120 g 9.6 mg 0.24 ml
140 g 11.2 mg 0.28 ml

Gentamicin, 80 mg/kg (Mice) 20 g 1.6 mg 0.04 ml
25 g 2.0 mg 0.05 ml
30 g 2.4 mg 0.06 ml

The above table shows that animals with higher body weight will receive higher doses (mg or ml) of gentamicin injection.

However, twice serial dilution may be carried out to obtain larger measurable volumes, especially for mice.

Example of double dilution:
1 ml gentamicin + 1 ml normal saline → 20 mg/ml
Further dilution can be done to achieve measurable volume.

Therefore, double volume (e.g., 0.1 ml instead of 0.05 ml for 25 g mouse) can be administered from diluted stock.

Further serial dilution (3–4 times) may be required for very small animals to ensure accurate measurement.

7. Direct Calculation of Animals’ Dose from Human Dose

Some experimental situations may arise when animal doses of compounds of different formulations (syrup, tonic, solution, ampoule injection, suspension and tablet) are calculated from average adult (70 kg) human dose.

We shall consider chemiron blood tonic (figure 3) in this instance.

Figure 3: Chemiron Blood Tonic

For example, if the required dose of chemiron blood tonic for a 70 kg human = 10 ml (×3 daily) = 30 ml/70 kg/day, dosage for a rat weighing 150 g can be calculated as follows:

70 kg (70,000 g) adult man requires = 30 ml of chemiron blood tonic per day

Therefore,

150 g rat requires = (30 ml / 70,000 g) × 150 g

= 0.06 ml

Since the volume (0.06 ml) is very small to be measured accurately with a one milliliter syringe, it is recommended to carry out ten times (×10) serial dilution for the tonic.

Serial Dilution Method:

Dilute 1 ml of chemiron blood tonic with 9 ml of normal saline

OR

Dilute 10 ml of chemiron blood tonic with 90 ml of normal saline

Required new volume of chemiron tonic for 150 g rat from the diluted stock will be:

0.06 ml × 10 = 0.6 ml

Subsequent volume of chemiron can be calculated for rats whose body weights differ from 150 g using the same proportional formula.

However, further serial dilutions (×15 to ×20) are recommended for mice (which weigh far less than rats) in order to obtain an accurately measurable and administrable volume.

8. Restraining & Administration

Oral administration requires gentle restraint and gavage tube insertion via esophagus.

Intraperitoneal injection involves administration into abdominal cavity avoiding visceral organs.

Proper training minimizes stress, prevents injury and ensures experimental accuracy.
