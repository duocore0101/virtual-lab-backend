Subcutaneous (SC) – Technique

  • Restrain animal by scruffing (mice) or firm restraint (rats).
  • Swab injection site with 70% alcohol.
  • Pinch/tent loose skin to create subcutaneous space.
  • Insert needle bevel-up at 15–45° angle.
  • Aspirate briefly (no blood confirms placement).
  • Inject slowly; visible bleb confirms correct SC delivery.
  • Withdraw needle; no massage required.

Special Application: Osmotic minipumps may be implanted subcutaneously for long-term (7–28 days) continuous drug delivery.

Subcutaneous (SC) Route

Overview

The subcutaneous route involves injection into the loose connective tissue beneath the dermis. It is widely used for moderate volumes, sustained absorption, and depot formulations in laboratory animals.

Advantages & Disadvantages

  • Advantages: Easy, safe, prolonged release.
  • Low Risk: Less chance of damaging vital organs.
  • Disadvantages: Slower onset than IV/IP.
  • Risk: Local irritation or abscess formation.
  • Absorption: Influenced by blood flow and temperature.

Equipment & Pharmacokinetics

  • Syringe: 0.5–1 mL (mice), 1–3 mL (rats).
  • Needle: 25–30G, ½–1 inch length.
  • Onset: 15–30 minutes.
  • No first-pass metabolism.
  • Bioavailability often near 100% (water-soluble drugs).
  • Recommended Volume: Mice 10–20 mL/kg; Rats 5–10 mL/kg per site.
