Intraperitoneal (IP) – Technique

  • Restrain mouse by scruffing; tilt head downward (45°).
  • Disinfect lower abdominal quadrant.
  • Insert needle at 15–30° angle (avoid midline).
  • Aspirate gently to ensure no blood/urine/feces.
  • Inject slowly and withdraw needle carefully.
  • Monitor animal for distress or complications.

Note: Use smallest effective volume and maintain strict aseptic technique to prevent peritonitis.

Intraperitoneal (IP) Route

Overview

The intraperitoneal route involves injecting drugs directly into the peritoneal cavity. It is widely used in rodents for rapid systemic absorption and is ideal when oral bioavailability is poor.

Advantages & Disadvantages

  • Advantages: Simple, rapid absorption, larger volumes than IV/IM.
  • Suitable For: Acute and chronic dosing studies.
  • Disadvantages: Risk of organ puncture or peritonitis.
  • Absorption: Partial first-pass metabolism via portal vein.

Equipment & Pharmacokinetics

  • Syringe: 0.5–1 mL (mice), 1–5 mL (rats).
  • Needle: 25–27G (mice), 23–25G (rats).
  • Onset: 5–20 minutes.
  • Bioavailability: High but variable.
  • Recommended Volume: Mice 10–20 mL/kg; Rats 10–20 mL/kg.
