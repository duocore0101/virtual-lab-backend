Intramuscular (IM) – Technique

  • Anesthetize animal (isoflurane recommended for rodents).
  • Extend hind leg gently; restrain properly.
  • Disinfect injection site with alcohol.
  • Insert needle at 90° angle into caudal thigh muscle (3–5 mm depth in mice).
  • Aspirate to ensure no blood (avoid vascular injection).
  • Inject slowly; withdraw needle carefully.
  • Monitor for lameness or signs of nerve injury.

Important: Avoid sciatic nerve and use minimal volume to prevent muscle damage.

Intramuscular (IM) Route

Overview

The intramuscular route involves injecting drugs directly into muscle tissue for relatively rapid absorption. It is commonly used for vaccines, depot preparations, and moderately irritant drugs.

Advantages & Disadvantages

  • Advantages: Faster absorption than SC.
  • Suitable for depot/oil-based formulations.
  • No first-pass metabolism.
  • Disadvantages: Painful and stressful.
  • Risk of sciatic nerve damage or muscle necrosis.
  • Limited injection volume in small animals.

Equipment & Pharmacokinetics

  • Syringe: 0.5–1 mL (precision).
  • Needle: 23–27G, ½–1 inch length.
  • Onset: 10–30 minutes.
  • High bioavailability for aqueous solutions.
  • Recommended Volume: Mice ≤0.05 mL; Rats ≤0.3 mL per site.
  • Preferred Sites: Caudal thigh or gastrocnemius muscle.
