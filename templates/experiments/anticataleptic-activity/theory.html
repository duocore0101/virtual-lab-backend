Theory – Anti-Catatonic (Anti-Cataleptic) Activity

Principle

Catalepsy is a behavioral condition characterized by immobility, muscle rigidity, and maintenance of imposed posture. In experimental pharmacology, catalepsy is commonly produced by typical antipsychotic drugs.

Drugs such as haloperidol block dopamine D2 receptors in the nigrostriatal pathway, resulting in extrapyramidal side effects (EPS). This experimentally induced catalepsy closely resembles Parkinsonism and catatonia observed in humans.

Pharmacological Basis

  • Typical Antipsychotics: Drugs like haloperidol and perphenazine induce catalepsy by blocking dopamine receptors in the nigrostriatal pathway.
  • Anti-Catatonic Drugs: Anticholinergic drugs (e.g., scopolamine, trihexyphenidyl) and dopaminergic agents reduce catalepsy by restoring the balance between dopamine and acetylcholine in the brain.
  • Atypical Antipsychotics: These drugs produce minimal or no catalepsy, indicating a lower liability for extrapyramidal side effects.

Significance

The anti-catatonic activity model is widely used to evaluate the extrapyramidal side-effect liability of antipsychotic drugs and to screen anti-parkinsonian agents.

A significant reduction in cataleptic score or duration by a test drug indicates potential anti-catatonic or anti-parkinsonian activity and suggests a safer pharmacological profile.
