Theory/Principle

Principle

Drugs can be tested on isolated preparations of experimental animals such as frog’s heart. Even though the frog’s heart differs from the mammalian heart, the fundamental properties of cardiac muscle are common to both.

Frog’s heart is sturdier than the mammalian heart and hence is widely used for experimental purposes.

Structure of Frog’s Heart

Frog’s heart consists of three chambers: two atria and one ventricle. The sinus venosus of the frog’s heart acts as the pacemaker.

Impulses generated from the sinus venosus spread over both atria and ventricle, producing coordinated contractions.

Recording of Cardiac Activity

With the help of Starling’s heart lever as a recording device, one can easily record the normal activity of frog’s heart and also study the effect of drugs on it.

The heart contractions are recorded in the form of waves showing force of contraction against time.

Normal Activity of Frog’s Heart

The normal activity of frog’s heart is represented by rhythmic contractions with a constant heart rate and force of contraction.

The height of the wave indicates the force of contraction, while the frequency of waves represents heart rate (beats per minute).

Effect of Drugs on Frog’s Heart

Drugs may increase or decrease heart rate and force of contraction. Increase in heart rate and force of contraction is termed as positive chronotropic and positive inotropic effects respectively.

Decrease in heart rate and force of contraction is termed as negative chronotropic and negative inotropic effects.

Drugs showing positive effects may be useful in bradycardia, whereas drugs showing negative effects may be useful in tachycardia.
