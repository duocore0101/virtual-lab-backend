Tail Flick Analgesiometer

Overview

The Tail Flick Analgesiometer, also known as the Tail-Flick Analgesia Meter or Radiant Heat Tail Flick Test Apparatus, is a widely used instrument in experimental pharmacology for evaluating analgesic drugs.

It measures the latency of the tail-flick reflex in response to a focused thermal stimulus, thereby assessing spinal nociceptive reflex pathways. The test is especially sensitive to opioid analgesics.

Principle

A focused beam of radiant heat (or hot water immersion in certain variants) is applied to the animal’s tail. This thermal stimulus activates nociceptive afferents and triggers a rapid spinal reflex known as the tail flick or tail withdrawal response.

Analgesic drugs prolong the latency to this reflex. The tail-flick test is more sensitive to strong opioid analgesics (e.g., morphine) than the hot plate test and can detect both central and some peripheral analgesics (D’Amour & Smith, 1941).

Description of the Instrument

  • Stimulus Source: High-intensity halogen lamp or infrared source providing focused radiant heat.
  • Intensity Control: Adjustable control (e.g., rheostat) to set baseline reaction latency, typically 3–5 seconds.
  • Animal Restrainer: Grooved holder that immobilizes the body while allowing exposure of the tail.
  • Detection System: Photocells or motion sensors detect tail movement and automatically stop the timer.
  • Timer & Display: Digital timer with automatic cut-off (usually 10–15 seconds) to prevent tissue damage.
  • Modern Features: Foot switch operation, data output interfaces, and precise focusing slit for consistent heat application.

Applications

  • Screening of opioid and non-opioid analgesic drugs
  • Assessment of spinal reflex-mediated nociception
  • Comparison of analgesic potency and duration of action
  • Preclinical pain research
  • Teaching experimental pharmacology practicals
