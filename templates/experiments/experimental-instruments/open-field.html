Open Field Apparatus (Open Field Test Arena)

Overview

The Open Field Apparatus, also known as the Open Field Test or Open Field Maze, is a fundamental behavioral instrument used in experimental pharmacology and neuroscience to assess general locomotor activity, exploratory behavior, and anxiety-like behavior in rodents.

It is a simple and versatile test that measures spontaneous activity when an animal is placed in a novel, open environment.

Principle

Rodents naturally explore new environments but also exhibit thigmotaxis, a preference for staying close to the walls due to fear of open spaces. The open field creates a conflict between the central area (exposed, aversive) and the peripheral zone (safer).

Key behavioral parameters include:

  • Locomotor Activity: Total distance traveled, ambulation, and line crossings.
  • Anxiety: Reduced time or entries into the center indicate higher anxiety; anxiolytics increase center exploration.
  • Exploration: Rearing, grooming behavior, and defecation (emotionality index).

CNS stimulants increase activity, while CNS depressants decrease it. Anxiolytics selectively increase center exploration without significantly altering total locomotion.

Description of the Instrument

  • Arena: Large square or circular open box without a roof.
  • Dimensions (for mice): Typically 40–50 cm × 40–50 cm, with walls 30–40 cm high.
  • Material: Opaque acrylic or Perspex (white, black, or gray) to minimize reflections.
  • Zones: Virtually divided into a central zone (e.g., 20 × 20 cm) and a peripheral outer zone.
  • Features: Even illumination (approximately 50–100 lux) and testing in a quiet room to reduce stress.
  • Modern Setups: Infrared beams, overhead video cameras, and automated tracking software (e.g., ANY-maze, EthoVision) for precise measurement of path tracing, zone time, and movement velocity.

Applications

  • Assessment of locomotor and exploratory activity
  • Screening of anxiolytic and anxiogenic drugs
  • Evaluation of CNS stimulant and depressant effects
  • Behavioral neuroscience and pharmacology research
  • Teaching experimental pharmacology practicals
