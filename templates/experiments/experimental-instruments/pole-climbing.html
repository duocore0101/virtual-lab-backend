Pole Climbing Apparatus (Cook’s)

Overview

The Pole Climbing Apparatus, commonly known as Cook’s Pole Climbing Apparatus, is a behavioral instrument used in experimental pharmacology to evaluate antipsychotic (neuroleptic) drugs using the conditioned avoidance response (CAR) test.

It distinguishes the selective suppression of avoidance behavior produced by antipsychotics from the non-specific motor suppression caused by sedative drugs.

Principle

Animals are trained to climb a vertical pole in response to a conditioned stimulus (CS), such as a buzzer sound, to avoid an unconditioned stimulus (US) in the form of a mild electric foot shock.

Antipsychotic drugs (e.g., chlorpromazine) selectively block the avoidance response (climbing during CS before shock) without affecting the escape response (climbing after shock onset). Sedatives and non-specific CNS depressants suppress both responses.

This selective effect models the therapeutic suppression of psychotic symptoms without producing excessive sedation.

Description of the Instrument

  • Chamber: Transparent Perspex or acrylic box with a grid floor for delivery of electric shock and a sliding door.
  • Pole: Vertical wooden or metal pole fixed at the center or lid of the chamber, providing a safe climbing area.
  • Stimuli:
    • Audible buzzer (CS), typically ~50 Hz tone
    • Electric shock generator (US), 0.5–2 mA, scrambled
  • Controls:
    • Timer for CS duration (10–15 seconds)
    • Shock intensity control
    • Counters for avoidance, escape, and failure responses
  • Modern Features: Digital timers, automated response detection, and software-based data analysis.

Applications

  • Screening of antipsychotic (neuroleptic) drugs
  • Evaluation of conditioned avoidance behavior
  • Distinguishing antipsychotic effects from sedation
  • Behavioral pharmacology research
  • Teaching experimental pharmacology practicals
