Eddy’s Hot Plate Analgesiometer

Overview

Eddy’s Hot Plate Analgesiometer, commonly known as the Hot Plate Test, is a classical instrument in experimental pharmacology used to evaluate the central analgesic (pain-relieving) activity of drugs, particularly those acting at supraspinal levels.

It is especially useful for assessing opioid analgesics such as morphine and for differentiating central from peripheral analgesic action.

Principle

The test is based on the animal’s behavioral response to heat-induced pain. When mice or rats are placed on a heated surface (typically 55 ± 0.5°C), they exhibit nociceptive responses such as paw licking, paw withdrawal, or jumping.

Centrally acting analgesic drugs delay the onset of these responses by acting on central pain pathways (e.g., opioid receptors). In contrast, peripheral analgesics such as NSAIDs show little or no effect, allowing differentiation between central and peripheral analgesic mechanisms.

Description of the Instrument

  • Heating Plate: Copper or aluminum plate (usually 20–30 cm diameter) providing uniform heat distribution.
  • Temperature Control: Adjustable thermostat (50–60°C), commonly maintained at 55°C for mice.
  • Enclosure: Transparent Perspex cylinder or box to confine the animal while preventing escape.
  • Timer: Digital stopwatch or automated timer to record reaction latency.
  • Safety Cut-off: Automatic cut-off time (usually 30–60 seconds) to prevent tissue damage.
  • Modern Features: Digital temperature display, response-triggered sensors, or foot pedal for manual timing.

Applications

  • Screening centrally acting analgesic drugs
  • Evaluation of opioid analgesics
  • Differentiation of central vs. peripheral analgesic action
  • Assessment of pain threshold and nociception
  • Teaching experimental pharmacology practicals
