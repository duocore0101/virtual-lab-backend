Staircase Apparatus (Staircase Test)

Overview

The Staircase Apparatus, also known as the Staircase Test, is a behavioral instrument used in experimental pharmacology for screening anxiolytic (anti-anxiety) drugs, primarily in mice.

It allows simultaneous assessment of anxiety-like behavior and locomotor/exploratory activity in a novel environment, making it a simple and rapid test that does not require prior training.

Principle

The staircase test exploits neophobia (fear of novelty) in rodents. When placed in a novel enclosed staircase, naive animals explore the environment by climbing steps and rearing on their hind legs.

Anxiolytic drugs (e.g., benzodiazepines) selectively reduce rearing behavior (an index of anxiety) at doses that do not reduce, and may even increase, step climbing (locomotor activity). In contrast, non-specific sedatives suppress both behaviors.

This enables differentiation of true anxiolytics from general CNS depressants. (Note: Other staircase tests exist for motor function, but this version refers specifically to anxiety screening.)

Description of the Instrument

  • Chamber: Enclosed Perspex or acrylic box containing an internal staircase.
  • Staircase: Typically consists of 5 steps arranged sequentially inside the chamber.
  • Dimensions (for mice):
    • Box: approximately 40–50 cm long
    • Width/Height: approximately 10–15 cm
    • Step height: approximately 5–10 cm
  • Features: Opaque or transparent walls; no food bait (unlike motor staircase tests).
  • Observation: Manual counting or video recording of behaviors over a 3–5 minute test period.

Applications

  • Screening of anxiolytic drugs
  • Assessment of anxiety-like behavior
  • Evaluation of exploratory and locomotor activity
  • Behavioral pharmacology research
  • Teaching experimental pharmacology practicals
