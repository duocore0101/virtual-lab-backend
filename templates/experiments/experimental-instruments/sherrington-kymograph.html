Sherrington's Rotating Drum (Kymograph)

Overview

Sherrington's Rotating Drum, also known as the Sherrington's Kymograph, Student Kymograph, or Recording Drum, is a traditional recording instrument used in experimental pharmacology to graphically record physiological responses such as muscle contractions and heart beats over time.

Popularized by Sir Charles Sherrington, it remains a core teaching tool in undergraduate pharmacology practicals, especially for in vitro isolated tissue experiments.

Principle

A smoked paper (kymograph paper) is tightly wrapped around a rotating cylindrical drum. A lever or stylus connected to the tissue writes on the smoked paper as the drum rotates at a constant speed, producing a time-based tracing of contractions or relaxations.

By changing the rotation speed using gears, both slow and fast physiological events can be recorded. In modern digital kymographs, smoked paper is replaced by sensors and computer software.

Description of the Instrument

  • Drum / Cylinder: Vertical brass or stainless steel drum (height ~15–20 cm, diameter ~15 cm) with interchangeable gears allowing speeds from approximately 0.12 to 640 mm/sec.
  • Motor: Electrically driven motor with 8–10 speed gearbox, start/stop clutch, and neutral position.
  • Base: Heavy cast iron base to provide mechanical stability and vibration-free recording.
  • Accessories:
    • Smoked kymograph paper
    • Varnish for fixing tracings
    • Frontal writing levers (isotonic or isometric)
    • Tuning fork for time marking
  • Digital Variants: Sensor-based systems with direct computer recording, eliminating the need for smoked paper.

Applications

  • Recording isolated tissue contractions
  • Studying drug effects on smooth and cardiac muscle
  • Demonstration of agonist and antagonist responses
  • Time-response relationship analysis
  • Teaching experimental pharmacology practicals
