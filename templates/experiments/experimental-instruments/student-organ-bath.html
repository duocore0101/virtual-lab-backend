Student Organ Bath

Overview

The Student Organ Bath (also known as the Isolated Tissue Bath or Physiological Organ Bath) is a fundamental instrument in experimental pharmacology used for in vitro studies on isolated smooth muscle tissues such as guinea pig ileum, rat uterus, rabbit jejunum, and frog rectus abdominis.

It maintains isolated tissues in a viable state under controlled physiological conditions to record drug-induced contractions or relaxations.

Principle

The isolated tissue is suspended in a nutrient-rich physiological salt solution such as Tyrode’s or Ringer’s solution maintained at 37°C. The solution is continuously aerated with carbogen (95% O₂ + 5% CO₂) to supply oxygen and maintain physiological pH.

Drugs are added directly into the organ bath, and the tissue response is recorded isometrically or isotonically using a lever or force transducer connected to a kymograph or digital recording system.

Description of the Instrument

  • Bath Chamber: Inner glass tube (5–20 ml capacity) surrounded by an outer jacket for circulating warm water.
  • Temperature Regulation: Thermostatically controlled water bath or heater maintains 37°C.
  • Aeration: Fine tube for continuous bubbling of carbogen.
  • Tissue Holder: Lower hook or clip holds tissue; upper end connected to lever or force transducer.
  • Recording System:
    • Traditional: Frontal writing lever with smoked kymograph drum
    • Modern: Isometric transducer with digital polygraph/software
  • Accessories: Mariotte bottle for constant solution level and overflow outlet for washing the tissue.

Applications

  • Study of smooth muscle pharmacology
  • Construction of dose–response curves
  • Agonist–antagonist interaction studies
  • Receptor characterization and pharmacodynamics
  • Teaching experimental pharmacology practicals
