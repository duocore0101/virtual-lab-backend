Actophotometer (Activity Cage)

Overview

The Actophotometer, also known as an activity cage or locomotor activity meter, is a standard instrument in experimental pharmacology used to measure spontaneous locomotor activity in laboratory rodents such as mice and rats.

It is widely used for screening central nervous system (CNS) acting drugs, where locomotor activity serves as an index of alertness, wakefulness, and CNS excitability.

Principle

The actophotometer operates on the principle of photoelectric cell interruption. Infrared or visible light beams are arranged around a square or circular arena. When the animal moves and interrupts a beam, the photocell detects the interruption and registers it as a count on a digital counter.

Increased movement results in higher activity counts. CNS depressants (e.g., diazepam, chlorpromazine) reduce the counts, whereas CNS stimulants (e.g., caffeine, amphetamine) increase the activity score.

Description of the Instrument

  • Chamber: Transparent Perspex (acrylic) cage, usually square in shape, sometimes provided with a grid floor.
  • Sensors: Typically 6 built-in photocells or infrared beams arranged along the sides of the cage.
  • Display: 4-digit digital counter displaying total activity counts.
  • Additional Features:
    • Reset button for zeroing counts
    • Timer (commonly 5–10 minutes)
    • Optional mild electric shock stimulator (up to 100 V, low current) in older models
  • Modern Versions: Fully digital systems providing precise, automated counting without manual intervention.

Applications

  • Screening of CNS stimulant and depressant drugs
  • Evaluation of sedative and anxiolytic agents
  • Assessment of drug-induced hyperactivity or sedation
  • Behavioral pharmacology research
  • Teaching experimental pharmacology practicals
