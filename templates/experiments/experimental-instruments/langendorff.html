Langendorff Apparatus

Overview

The Langendorff Apparatus, also known as the Langendorff Isolated Heart Perfusion System, is a classic ex vivo instrument used in experimental pharmacology and cardiovascular research for studying the isolated perfused heart.

It enables detailed evaluation of cardiac function such as contractility, heart rate, rhythm, and coronary flow independent of systemic influences, making it ideal for studying cardioactive drugs and myocardial injury.

Principle

Named after Oskar Langendorff (1895), this system is based on retrograde perfusion of the isolated heart. Oxygenated physiological solution (commonly Krebs–Henseleit buffer) is pumped into the aorta, causing the aortic valve to close and directing the perfusate backward into the coronary arteries.

The heart beats spontaneously (or can be electrically paced) in the absence of external workload (non-working mode). Alternatively, the system can be configured to a working heart mode, where the heart ejects against defined preload and afterload pressures.

Drugs added to the perfusate directly act on the myocardium, allowing precise assessment of cardiac pharmacodynamics.

Perfusion modes:

  • Constant pressure: Physiological mode with variable flow
  • Constant flow: Fixed flow with variable perfusion pressure

Description of the Instrument

  • Reservoir / Jacketed Oxygenator: Maintains perfusate at 37°C and continuously bubbles carbogen (95% O₂ + 5% CO₂) to ensure oxygenation and pH stability.
  • Peristaltic Pump: Provides controlled delivery of perfusate at constant flow rates.
  • Aortic Cannula: Site where the excised heart is mounted for retrograde perfusion.
  • Sensors:
    • Pressure transducers (aortic and left ventricular)
    • Flow meters for coronary flow measurement
    • Force transducer or intraventricular balloon for contractility
  • Modern Features: Integrated temperature control, pH monitoring, bubble traps, and computerized data acquisition software.
  • Variants: Working heart systems and compact student-friendly versions for teaching laboratories.

Applications

  • Evaluation of cardioactive and antiarrhythmic drugs
  • Study of myocardial contractility and coronary circulation
  • Ischemia–reperfusion injury research
  • Assessment of myocardial preservation techniques
  • Teaching experimental pharmacology and cardiovascular physiology
