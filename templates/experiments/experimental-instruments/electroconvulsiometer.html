Electroconvulsiometer (Convulsiometer)

Overview

The Electroconvulsiometer, also called a Convulsiometer, is a specialized instrument in experimental pharmacology used to induce controlled electrical seizures in laboratory animals such as mice or rats.

It is primarily employed for screening anticonvulsant drugs by modeling generalized tonic–clonic (grand mal) seizures.

Principle

The test is based on the delivery of a supramaximal electroshock (MES) through corneal or ear clip electrodes. This electrical stimulus induces a characteristic seizure pattern, with tonic hindlimb extension (THE) serving as the primary endpoint.

Effective anticonvulsant drugs (e.g., phenytoin, carbamazepine) abolish or significantly reduce the duration of tonic hindlimb extension, indicating protection against seizure spread.

The MES test is highly predictive for drugs effective in treating generalized tonic–clonic seizures in humans.

Description of the Instrument

  • Power Supply: Adjustable parameters including current (mA), voltage, frequency (typically 50–60 Hz), and shock duration (approximately 0.1–1 second).
  • Electrodes: Corneal electrodes or padded ear clip electrodes; saline or electrode gel is used to ensure proper electrical conductivity.
  • Controls: Digital display for stimulus parameters, timer control, and optional force stimulator.
  • Safety Features: Constant current source to deliver a consistent stimulus despite variations in animal resistance.
  • Modern Features: Programmable stimulation protocols, data logging, automatic cut-off, and enhanced safety controls.

Applications

  • Screening of anticonvulsant and antiepileptic drugs
  • Evaluation of seizure severity and seizure spread
  • Preclinical epilepsy research
  • Pharmacological profiling of CNS-active drugs
  • Teaching experimental pharmacology practicals
