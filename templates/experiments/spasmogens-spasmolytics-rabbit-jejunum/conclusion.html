Result & Conclusion

Result

The isolated rabbit jejunum preparation showed characteristic responses to different pharmacological agents affecting intestinal smooth muscle.

  • Acetylcholine (ACh) produced a marked increase in tone and amplitude of contraction due to stimulation of muscarinic (M3) receptors.
  • Barium chloride (BaCl₂) caused sustained contraction of smooth muscle due to direct depolarization of the muscle membrane.
  • Atropine produced relaxation by blocking muscarinic receptors (antimuscarinic effect).
  • Adrenaline decreased intestinal contractions through β₂ adrenergic receptor mediated smooth muscle relaxation.
  • Pre-treatment with atropine significantly reduced the contractile response produced by acetylcholine, demonstrating competitive antagonism.

Conclusion

Spasmogens such as acetylcholine (acting through muscarinic receptors) and barium chloride (acting directly on smooth muscle) increase intestinal motility and produce contraction of the rabbit jejunum.

Spasmolytic agents reduce smooth muscle activity by different mechanisms:

  • Atropine produces relaxation by blocking muscarinic receptors.
  • Adrenaline relaxes smooth muscle via β₂ adrenergic receptor activation.
  • Papaverine acts as a non-specific smooth muscle relaxant through phosphodiesterase inhibition and calcium channel blockade.

This experiment demonstrates the differential pharmacological actions of spasmogenic and spasmolytic drugs on intestinal smooth muscle.

Such studies are useful in the screening and evaluation of antispasmodic drugs used in clinical conditions such as intestinal colic and irritable bowel syndrome (IBS).
