Theory/Principle

Principle

The isolated rabbit jejunum shows spontaneous rhythmic contractions due to intrinsic activity of intestinal smooth muscle.

These contractions can be modified by drugs that either increase or decrease smooth muscle activity.

  • Spasmogens increase intestinal smooth muscle contraction.
  • Acetylcholine (ACh) acts on muscarinic (M3) receptors producing cholinergic stimulation and contraction.
  • Barium chloride (BaCl₂) acts directly on smooth muscle causing membrane depolarization and contraction.

Action of Spasmolytics

Spasmolytics reduce intestinal contractions by relaxing smooth muscle or blocking stimulatory pathways.

  • Atropine – competitive muscarinic antagonist that blocks the action of acetylcholine.
  • Adrenaline – acts on β₂ adrenergic receptors producing relaxation of intestinal smooth muscle and inhibition of motility.
  • Papaverine – a non-specific smooth muscle relaxant that inhibits phosphodiesterase and blocks calcium entry, resulting in muscle relaxation.

The responses of the tissue are recorded as changes in spontaneous contractions using a kymograph or student physiograph.
