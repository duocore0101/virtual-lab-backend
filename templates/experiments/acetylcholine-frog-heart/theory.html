Theory / Principle

Principle

Acetylcholine (ACh) binds to muscarinic receptors (primarily M2 subtype) present on cardiac muscle cells, producing inhibitory effects on the heart.

  • Negative chronotropic effect: Decrease in heart rate (bradycardia)
  • Negative inotropic effect: Decrease in force of contraction (reduced amplitude)

This occurs through the following mechanism:

  1. Activation of M2 receptors → coupling to Gi proteins → inhibition of adenylyl cyclase → reduced intracellular cAMP levels.
  2. Opening of G-protein–gated inward rectifier K+ channels leads to membrane hyperpolarization, slowing pacemaker activity (especially in the sinus venosus, the pacemaker of the frog heart) and reducing cardiac excitability.
  3. At higher doses, acetylcholine may cause temporary cardiac arrest in diastole, which is reversible on washing with Ringer’s solution.

Cholinergic Action on Heart

Acetylcholine is a major neurohumoral transmitter at autonomic and somatic sites.

Acetylcholine, like parasympathomimetics, influences heart rate and force of contraction.

Acetylcholine acts through muscarinic receptors located in the heart.

Effect on Pacemaker Cells

Acetylcholine hyperpolarizes the pacemaker cells and decreases the rate of diastolic depolarization.

Thus, the rate of impulse generation is reduced, leading to bradycardia.

Effect on Cardiac Contractility

Acetylcholine reduces atrial and ventricular contractility by acting on cardiac muscle.

Acetylcholine increases K⁺ permeability, which leads to hyperpolarization and decrease in contractility.
