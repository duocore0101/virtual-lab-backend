Theory/Principle

Background of Psychiatric Disorders

The nosology (science of classification of diseases) makes a basic distinction among psychoses, cognitive disorders, mood disorders, anxiety disorders and disorders of personality.

Antipsychotic drugs are used primarily in the management of patients with psychotic and other psychiatric illnesses characterized by agitation and impaired reasoning.

It must be understood that the essential characteristics of human mental disorders cannot be completely reproduced in animals. Therefore, cognition, communication and social behavior in animals are difficult to compare directly with human conditions.

Chlorpromazine is widely used in the treatment of psychiatric disorders. It reduces psychomotor excitement, alters manic states and is used in the treatment of schizophrenia. It suppresses spontaneous movements and complex behaviors while spinal reflexes remain intact.

Antipsychotic Drug Action on Central Nervous System

Taming Effect of Chlorpromazine

In humans, chlorpromazine reduces initiative and interest in the environment, decreases emotional expression, slows responses to external stimuli and produces drowsiness. The patient becomes less agitated and calmer.

Aggressive and impulsive behavior diminishes. Delusions and hallucinations gradually disappear, which is referred to as the “taming effect”.

The psychophysiological and behavioral effects produced in animals by chlorpromazine show remarkable similarity to those seen in humans when administered in low doses. The drug reduces excitement while spinal reflexes remain unchanged.

At higher doses chlorpromazine produces cataleptic immobility in animals. Muscle tone increases, ptosis occurs and the animal becomes indifferent to external stimuli.

Apart from its neuroleptic effects, chlorpromazine also exhibits antiemetic and antihistaminic actions and can potentiate analgesics, sedatives and general anesthetics.

This tranquilizing and neuroleptic property of chlorpromazine is known as ataractic activity.

Behavioral Changes Produced by Chlorpromazine

Experimental Evaluation in Animals

The ataractic activity of chlorpromazine is greater than its sedative or anesthetic effect. The drug produces tranquilization (taming or calmness) without deep sedation or loss of alertness.

The taming and potentiating effects of chlorpromazine can be studied experimentally in laboratory animals such as mice and rats.

The effects can be observed by evaluating:

  • Conditioned avoidance response
  • Animal behavioral changes
  • Potentiation of CNS depressants
  • Reduction in spontaneous motor activity

The responses can be studied by different experimental methods:

  • Righting Reflex Method – Chlorpromazine potentiates barbiturate sleeping time in rodents.
  • Pole Climbing Method – Used to study conditioned avoidance response.
  • Actophotometer Method – Measures reduction in spontaneous motor activity.
  • Hot Plate Method – Demonstrates potentiation of analgesic activity.
  • Antagonism to stimulant drugs.
  • Suppression of aggressive or combative behavior.
