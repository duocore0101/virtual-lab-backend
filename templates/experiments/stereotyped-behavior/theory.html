Theory: Stereotyped Behavior Produced by Centrally Acting Drugs

Definition of Stereotyped Behavior

Stereotyped behavior is defined as repetitive, uniform, and purposeless motor activity that is performed continuously without any apparent goal. These behaviors are easily observable in laboratory animals following the administration of certain centrally acting drugs.

Neurochemical Basis

The development of stereotyped behavior is primarily associated with excessive stimulation of dopaminergic pathways in the central nervous system, particularly within the striatum. Activation of dopamine D1 and D2 receptors plays a key role in the expression of repetitive motor responses.

Role of Apomorphine and Other Drugs

Apomorphine is a direct dopamine receptor agonist that produces marked stereotyped behavior in rodents when administered in appropriate doses. Other stimulants, such as amphetamine, may also induce similar effects by increasing dopamine release in the brain.

Effect of Antipsychotic Drugs

Antipsychotic drugs such as chlorpromazine and haloperidol antagonize dopamine receptors and thereby suppress stereotyped behavior. The ability of a test drug to reduce these behaviors is considered indicative of potential antipsychotic activity.

Pharmacological Significance

This experimental model is widely used as a screening method for evaluating antipsychotic drugs. Suppression of stereotypy suggests dopamine antagonism in the mesolimbic system, which is therapeutically relevant in the treatment of psychotic disorders.
