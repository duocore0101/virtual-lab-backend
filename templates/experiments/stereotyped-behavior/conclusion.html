Conclusion

Result

Administration of apomorphine produced a marked increase in stereotyped behavior in the experimental animal when compared to the basal observation. The stereotypy score after drug administration was significantly higher than the baseline score.

Inference

Apomorphine, a dopamine receptor agonist, stimulates dopaminergic pathways in the central nervous system. This leads to enhanced repetitive motor activities such as sniffing, licking, and gnawing, which are collectively referred to as stereotyped behavior.

Conclusion

The experiment demonstrates that apomorphine significantly increases stereotyped behavior in mice, indicating its central dopaminergic stimulant activity. Hence, the study confirms the role of dopamine agonists in producing stereotypy and validates the experimental model used for evaluating CNS stimulant drugs.
