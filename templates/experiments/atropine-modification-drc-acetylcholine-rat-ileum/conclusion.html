Result & Conclusion

Result

The dose–response curve of acetylcholine (ACh) on isolated rat ileum smooth muscle shows a typical sigmoid-shaped curve when plotted against the logarithm of the dose.

Increasing doses of acetylcholine produce graded contractions of the ileum by stimulating muscarinic receptors present on the smooth muscle.

In the presence of atropine, acetylcholine produces smaller contractions at the same doses, and the dose–response curve shifts to the right side.

The maximum response remains nearly unchanged, indicating that atropine acts as a competitive antagonist.

Conclusion

Acetylcholine acts as an agonist that produces contraction of rat ileum smooth muscle through muscarinic (M3) receptors.

Atropine acts as a competitive muscarinic antagonist, blocking the receptor and requiring higher concentrations of acetylcholine to produce the same response.

This results in a parallel rightward shift of the dose–response curve without reducing the maximal response, which confirms competitive antagonism.

Thus, the experiment demonstrates that atropine blocks muscarinic receptors in smooth muscle, producing competitive antagonism against acetylcholine.
