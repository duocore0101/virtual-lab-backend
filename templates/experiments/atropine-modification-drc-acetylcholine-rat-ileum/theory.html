Theory / Principle

Principle of the Experiment

The rat ileum is a smooth muscle preparation containing muscarinic acetylcholine receptors, mainly the M3 subtype. When isolated and mounted in an organ bath, the tissue contracts in response to pharmacological agents acting on these receptors.

Action of Acetylcholine

Acetylcholine (ACh) acts as an agonist on muscarinic receptors present on the smooth muscle of the ileum. Activation of these receptors leads to an increase in intracellular calcium (Ca²⁺), which produces contraction of the smooth muscle in a concentration-dependent graded manner.

Role of Atropine

Atropine is a competitive antagonist of muscarinic receptors. It blocks the binding of acetylcholine to the receptor by occupying the same receptor site.

Competitive Antagonism

Because atropine competes with acetylcholine for the same receptor, higher concentrations of acetylcholine are required to produce the same response when atropine is present.

Effect on Dose Response Curve

In the presence of atropine, the dose-response curve of acetylcholine shifts to the right, indicating a decrease in potency of acetylcholine. Since atropine acts as a reversible competitive antagonist, the maximum response remains unchanged and the slope of the curve generally remains the same, producing a parallel rightward shift.
